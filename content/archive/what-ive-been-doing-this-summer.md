The summer of 2026 has been a blur of building, learning, and dodging monsoon showers in Pune. 
It has been one of the most productive stretches of my life, primarily because I’ve let myself jump between diverse projects without worrying about sticking to a single lane.

Here is a breakdown of what has been keeping me busy:

## 1. Building MedKundali

Our startup, **MedKundali**, is an attempt to solve a frustrating problem: the absolute chaos of medical documentation in India. 
Most patients carry around physical folders of prescriptions, test reports, and scan images. 
When they visit a new doctor, half their history is missing or illegible.

We are building a platform to digitize, index, and summarize these medical histories using lightweight OCR and local LLM pipelines. 
We want to give patients control over their health data and doctors a clean, instant summary of patient histories. 
This summer, I’ve been refining the dashboard interface and ensuring fast page load times under low-connectivity conditions.

## 2. Automating Academic Research with PaperFlow

As a physics student, reading academic papers is a daily chore. 
But papers are designed for print—dense layouts, formulas scattered across columns, and references tucked away at the bottom. 
I created **PaperFlow** to automate my reading pipeline.

It is a Python workflow that uses advanced OCR models and layout detection libraries to convert complex two-column PDFs into structured markdown. 
From there, I can run semantic searches, extract data tables, and generate summaries of experimental setups automatically. 
It has cut my research setup time in half.

## 3. Physics Studies

Aside from software, I’ve been reading up on statistical mechanics and quantum field theory. 
I'm trying to wrap my head around numerical simulations of spin systems, writing C++ scripts to run Monte Carlo simulations. 
There is something highly satisfying about watching a disordered system spontaneously align as the simulated temperature drops.

## What's Next?

As the summer starts to wind down, I want to deploy a few more of these prototypes, clean up my open-source code repositories, and write more of these short notes. 
This digital garden is a step in that direction—a place to log experiments, drafts, and thoughts in real time.
